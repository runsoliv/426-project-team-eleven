Project description:

Our system simulates an emergency weather alert network that coordinates severe weather warnings, emergency shelters, and community notifications during natural disasters such as hurricanes, tornadoes, and floods. A single server cannot handle the surge in requests and alerts during large-scale emergencies, when thousands of residents need real-time information at once.

Two regions illustrate who is most exposed. North Texas (the Dallas–Fort Worth area) sits within Tornado Alley and averages dozens of tornado warnings per year; residents of manufactured and mobile home communities there are especially vulnerable, since these structures fail at far lower wind speeds than site-built homes and residents often have only minutes to reach shelter. South Florida (the Miami–Fort Lauderdale corridor) faces frequent hurricanes and the flash flooding that follows them; low-lying coastal neighborhoods and residents without reliable transportation are most exposed, since evacuation orders and shelter locations must reach them quickly and accurately to be useful. In both regions, delayed or inaccurate alerts directly reduce the time people have to reach safety, and emergency responders rely on the same system to coordinate shelters and evacuation routes for these communities.
